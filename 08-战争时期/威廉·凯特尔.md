## Early life and career

Wilhelm Keitel was born in the village of Helmscherode near Gandersheim in the Duchy of Brunswick, Germany. He was the eldest son of Carl Keitel (1854–1934), a middle-class landowner, and his wife Apollonia Vissering (1855–1888). As a youngster his main interests were hunting and riding horses,[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeitel200013-2) hobbies which he pursued also later in life.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeitel2000189-3) He was also interested in farming[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeitel200013-2) and wanted to take over his family's estates after completing his education at a gymnasium "Gymnasium (Germany)"). This plan failed as his father did not want to retire. Instead, he embarked on a military career in 1901, becoming an officer cadet of the Prussian Army. As a commoner, he did not join the cavalry, but a field artillery regiment in Wolfenbüttel, serving as adjutant from 1908.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMitchamMueller20121-4) On 18 April 1909, Keitel married Lisa Fontaine, a wealthy landowner's daughter at Wülfel near Hanover.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoerlitz2003140-5)

Keitel was 1.85 metres (6feet 1inch) tall, later described as a solidly built and square-jawed Prussian.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargaritis201925-6)

During World War I, Keitel served on the Western Front "Western Front (World War I)") and took part in the fighting in Flanders, where he was severely wounded.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMitchamMueller20122-7) After being promoted to captain, Keitel was posted to the staff "Staff (military)") of an infantry division in 1915.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoerlitz2003140%E2%80%93141-8) After the war, Keitel was retained in the newly created _Reichswehr_ of the Weimar Republic and played a part in organizing the paramilitary _Freikorps_ units on the Polish border. In 1924, Keitel was transferred to the Ministry of the Reichswehr in Berlin, serving with the _Truppenamt_ ('Troop Office'), the post-Versailles disguised German General Staff. Three years later, he returned to field command.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMitchamMueller20122-7)

Now a lieutenant-colonel, Keitel was again assigned to the war ministry in 1929 and was soon promoted to Head of the Organizational Department ("T-2"), a post he held until Adolf Hitler took power in 1933. **Wilhelm Bodewin Johann Gustav Keitel** (German pronunciation:[\[ˈvɪlh

ɛlmˈkaɪtl̩\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German"); 22 September 1882–16 October 1946) was a German field marshal who held office as chief of the _Oberkommando der Wehrmacht_ (OKW), the high command of Nazi Germany's armed forces, during World War II. He signed a number of criminal orders "Criminal orders (Nazi Germany)") and directives that led to numerous war crimes.

Keitel's rise to the _Wehrmacht_ high command began with his appointment as the head of the Armed Forces Office at the Reich Ministry of War in 1935. Having taken command of the _Wehrmacht_ in 1938, Adolf Hitler replaced the ministry with the OKW and Keitel became its chief. He was reviled among his military colleagues as Hitler's habitual " yes-man".

After the war, Keitel was indicted by the International Military Tribunal in Nuremberg as one of the "major war criminals". He was found guilty on all counts of the indictment: crimes against humanity, crimes against peace, criminal conspiracy, and war crimes. He was sentenced to death and executed by hanging in 1946.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Keitel#cite_note-1)
